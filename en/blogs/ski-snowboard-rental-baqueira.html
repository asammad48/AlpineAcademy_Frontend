Rent or Buy? The Smartest Option

For many, the question of whether to rent or buy is one of the most important. The answer is simple: for most skiers, renting is the smartest option. Not only do you save a significant initial investment and annual maintenance costs, but you also have the freedom to try the latest generation equipment every season. Our partners constantly renew their equipment, which always guarantees you the best, without having to worry about transportation logistics or storage.

The Importance of Quality Equipment

Renting your equipment may seem simple, but the right choice makes a big difference in your performance and, above all, your safety on the slopes. Quality, modern, and well-maintained equipment offers multiple benefits. Therefore, we recommend renting from prestigious establishments that are also official distributors of leading brands, which ensures that their equipment is state-of-the-art and constantly renewed.

  • Greater safety: Modern skis or boards in good condition, with sharpened edges, smooth and well-waxed bases, and properly adjusted bindings, give you the control you need to avoid accidents.
  • Better progression: If you're a beginner, equipment adapted to your level helps you learn faster and with less effort. If you're an advanced skier, high-end gear allows you to perfect your technique and continue progressing, delivering better sensations in every turn or terrain you glide through.
  • Full enjoyment: Well-fitted boots and suitable equipment eliminate discomfort, allowing you to ski longer and enjoy every run without worries.

Selection Guide: The Key is in the Detail

Choosing your equipment is not a matter of luck, but of knowledge. Here are some expert tips so you know what to ask for at the shop. Of course, if we've already skied together, don't hesitate to let us know before renting or buying so we can advise you in a completely personalized way.

1. Skis: The Ultimate Guide by Level

Skis for Beginners: First Steps with Confidence

  • Length (Size): The golden rule is to choose skis 10 to 15 cm shorter than your height. This reduces weight in the tip and tail, making directional control and transition from one turn to another easier.
  • Turn Radius: Beginner skis usually have a shorter turn radius (between 12 and 15 meters). A short turn radius makes it easier to enter the turn without needing to exert much pressure or speed.
  • Weight and Response: They are lighter and made with softer materials. At low speeds, this flexibility absorbs irregularities and forgives technique errors, preventing the ski from reacting abruptly.
  • Waist (Width): The waist (the central part of the ski under the boot) is usually medium width (around 70-75 mm). This provides a stable support surface on groomed snow.

Skis for Intermediate Level: Gaining Ground and Speed

  • Length (Size): The recommended length is closer to the skier's height, typically between 5 and 10 cm shorter. This provides greater stability in wide turns and at higher speed.
  • Turn Radius: It sits in a medium range (between 14 and 17 meters). A slightly longer radius allows greater control in high-speed turns without sacrificing agility.
  • Weight and Response: They incorporate higher quality materials and a more robust construction. They may include a layer of carbon fiber or titanal to increase stiffness. This allows the ski to respond with more energy when exiting the turn.
  • Waist (Width): The waist remains at a similar or slightly larger width (75-85 mm), as at this level the skier ventures into a greater variety of terrain.

Skis for Advanced and Expert Level: Power, Precision and Specialization

  • Length and Ski Style: Size is no longer defined only by height, but by the chosen discipline: Slalom (SL) are shorter (between 155 and 165 cm), Giant Slalom (GS) are longer (between 175 and 195 cm), and Freeride/All-Mountain is a personal choice.
  • Waist Width: This factor defines the specialty: Narrow Waist (66-70 mm) for groomed runs, Medium Waist (70-86 mm) for All-Mountain, and Wide Waist (86-120 mm and more) for Freeride and powder snow.
  • Materials and Response: High-end skis incorporate double layers of Titanal, carbon and other fibers that increase their stiffness. They require refined technique to make them work, offering in return explosive power and response.

2. Boots: Your Connection with the Ski

The boot is the fundamental link that transmits every movement of your body to the snow. A perfect fit not only eliminates pain, but prevents injuries and guarantees maximum precision.

  • Boot Flex: The measure of stiffness. It ranges from 30 to 60 for children and beginners, 60 to 100 for intermediate levels, and 100 to 140+ for expert skiers. A higher flex translates into a faster and more powerful return when exiting the turn.
  • Last (Width): The width of the boot. A narrow last (97-98 mm) offers maximum precision, while a standard last (99-101 mm) balances comfort and control, and a wide last (102-103 mm) prioritizes comfort.
  • Rental vs. Purchase: Rental boots use softer plastics for durability, sacrificing some precision. Purchase boots, on the other hand, use harder plastics and thermoformable or heatable liners for instant and precise energy transmission.

Rental tip: always try several sizes and never choose a boot that's too big "for comfort": it compromises control and increases the risk of injuries.

3. Poles: Your Balance Tool

Pole height is vital for efficient technique. An incorrect measurement can subtly alter your position on the skis.

  • Standard Measure: Invert the pole, hold it below the basket and rest it on the ground. Your arm should form a 90-degree angle.
  • Short Poles: Facilitate tight turns and are ideal for mogul areas, allowing fluid movement.
  • Long Poles: Provide greater push on flat terrain and are useful for maintaining rhythm in wide turns.

4. Snowboard Equipment: Modality, Flex and Board Profile

When choosing a rental snowboard, it's important to focus on three key aspects: modality, flex (stiffness) and profile (camber or rocker).

Board flex

Flex is measured on a scale of 1 to 10:

  • Soft (1-4): Easy to handle and forgiving with mistakes. Recommended for beginners and freestyle in the snowpark.
  • Medium (4-7): Versatile and stable in all types of terrain. Ideal for "all-mountain" use.
  • Stiff (7-10): Very stable at high speed and with great grip on hard snow. Recommended for freeride and advanced riders.

Board profile (camber/rocker)

The profile determines the board's behavior on snow:

  • Camber (positive curvature): Greater grip and response, perfect for groomed runs and carving.
  • Rocker (negative curvature): Makes turns easier and floats better in powder snow. Ideal for beginners and freeride.
  • Hybrid (camber + rocker): Combines grip and flotation. It's the most versatile rental option.

Modality and recommended use

  • Groomed runs: More precise and fast boards, with good edge grip.
  • Freestyle: Twin boards, maneuverable and with softer flex for jumps and tricks.
  • Freeride: Directional boards, with longer nose and rocker in the tip to improve flotation in deep snow.

Practical tips for choosing the board at rental

  • Length: As a general rule, the board should reach between your chin and nose when placed upright next to your body. Shorter → easier to turn, recommended for beginners and freestyle. Longer → more stable at speed and with greater flotation in powder snow, ideal for freeride.
  • Width (waist width): Must fit boot size. Boots < EU 42 → standard board. Boots EU 43 or larger → wide board to prevent feet from hanging over and catching in the snow.
  • Experience level: Beginner → soft flex and rocker or simple hybrid. Intermediate → medium flex and hybrid. Advanced → medium-stiff flex and camber/hybrid depending on modality.

Quick rental summary

  • If you're a beginner → short board, soft flex, rocker or hybrid profile.
  • If you're looking for a versatile board → medium length, medium flex, hybrid profile.
  • If you want freeride or high-speed carving → longer board, stiff flex, camber or hybrid profile with rocker nose.
